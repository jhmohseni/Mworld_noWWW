The Hittites rose to power around the 18th century BCE in a place they called , which covers large parts of modern Turkey. By the 14th century BCE they had conquered almost all Asia Minor as well as parts of the northern Levant and Upper Mesopotamia.

The Hittite civilisation collapsed by the end of the 12th century BCE, not long after the Hittite King Hattusilis III fought at the battle of Kadesh against the Egyptian Pharaoh Rameses II. No-one really won the battle, but both opponents were seriously weakened.

The Hittites used a style of writing called cuneiform. Modern historians have decoded cuneiform, so we can guess what the Hittite language sounded like. Click on the link to hear this 3,000 year old language brought back to life!

Cuneiform was a writing script used by the Hittites and other ancient civilisations of the Mediterranean region. Cuneiform was based on writing signs on clay tablets. Each sign was made up of a number of wedge-shaped strokes. Each sign had the value of a syllable, such as "ka", "pe", and "ru". Other signs had specific meanings as words, such as "king".

Because writing involved memorizing so many signs, only a few people (called "scribes") could master this art. Therefore, scribes were in high demand in the palace of the Hittite kings. Today still, learning to read and write well is a very valuable skill!
